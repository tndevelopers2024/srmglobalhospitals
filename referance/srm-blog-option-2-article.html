Multiple sclerosis: What expert care at the right time can actually do.

The moment a patient hears the words "multiple sclerosis", the next decision they make is usually the wrong one. Not because they choose badly, but because they choose alone. The condition is treatable in ways that did not exist a decade ago, but the treatments only work if they begin before the damage accumulates. That window is shorter than most people realise.

Multiple sclerosis is an autoimmune condition where the body's immune system attacks the myelin sheath, the insulating cover around nerve fibres in the brain and spinal cord. The result is patchy, unpredictable nerve damage that can cause vision changes, numbness, weakness, balance problems, and cognitive fog.

2.8M
people live with multiple sclerosis globally. In India, the incidence is rising, partly because diagnosis is becoming more accurate. Most cases are diagnosed between ages 20 and 40.

The first six months matter more than the next six years

Here is the most important sentence in this article: the lesions you cannot feel are the ones doing the damage. By the time a patient experiences a clinical relapse, the MRI usually shows several silent lesions that occurred months earlier. Each one represents a small permanent loss. The job of treatment is not to manage relapses. It is to prevent the silent lesions in between them.

This is why early aggressive treatment with disease-modifying therapy has become the standard at most major neurology centres. The older approach was to start with a mild medication and escalate if the disease progressed. The newer approach, supported by large trials over the past five years, is to start strong and step down only if the patient is stable.

What disease-modifying therapy actually looks like

There are now over a dozen disease-modifying therapies available, ranging from oral medications taken daily to infusions given every six months. They fall broadly into three categories:

  • Platform therapies like interferons and glatiramer acetate. Older, well understood, modest efficacy.
  • Oral high-efficacy drugs like fingolimod, dimethyl fumarate, and cladribine.
  • Monoclonal antibody infusions like ocrelizumab, natalizumab, and ofatumumab. The strongest efficacy, given less frequently.

The choice depends on disease activity at presentation, lifestyle factors, family planning, and access. At SRM Global Hospitals, the decision is made in a multidisciplinary clinic that includes the patient's neurologist, an MS nurse, a pharmacist, and where relevant, a fertility consultant.

The second consultation, and the questions to ask

The first consultation usually focuses on confirming the diagnosis. The second consultation is where treatment decisions happen. Patients who come prepared with the right questions get better outcomes, because the conversation shifts from receiving information to making choices together.

Five questions for the second appointment

  1. What is my prognostic profile, based on my MRI and clinical features?
  2. Are we starting with a platform therapy or a high-efficacy drug, and why?
  3. What does the monitoring schedule look like for the first year?
  4. What is the plan if I want to start a family in the next three years?
  5. Who is my point of contact between consultations?

What recovery actually looks like

Recovery in MS is not a straight line. With early disease-modifying therapy, the goal is what neurologists call NEDA, which stands for No Evidence of Disease Activity. NEDA-3 means three things at once: no relapses, no new MRI lesions, and no progression on disability scales.

For patients who start treatment within six months of their first symptom, NEDA-3 is achievable in roughly 60 to 70 percent of cases. For patients who start treatment more than two years after their first symptom, that number falls below 30 percent. The drug has not changed. The biology has.

Life beyond the prescription

Disease-modifying therapy does its work in the background. The rest of life happens in front of the patient: vitamin D supplementation, regular cardiovascular exercise, smoking cessation if relevant, and mental health support. The MS clinic at SRM Global integrates a physiotherapist, an occupational therapist, and a clinical psychologist as standard, not as referrals.

If you or someone in your family has been recently diagnosed with multiple sclerosis, the most important next step is a second neurology consultation within thirty days.
